## Section 2: Six questions answered through praxeological reasoning

### Why ethical people build wealth slower — and what you're missing

Praxeology explains this cleanly through four mechanisms. First, **fraud is a production shortcut**: genuine entrepreneurship requires correctly anticipating future consumer demand and organizing production accordingly — an extremely difficult, time-consuming process. Fraud bypasses production entirely by simply taking existing value. As Rothbard wrote: "On the free market, everyone earns according to his productive value in satisfying consumer desires. Under statist distribution, everyone earns in proportion to the amount he can plunder from the producers."

Second, **externalized costs inflate apparent returns**: unethical actors shift costs onto others (pollution, broken contracts, exploited workers), making their "profit" appear higher per unit of effort. Their actual social contribution is zero or negative.

Third, **regulatory capture multiplies asymmetry**: Rothbard documented extensively that the most profitable unethical behavior isn't lone-wolf fraud but state-corporate nexus. "Attempts at cartelization without government intervention faltered time and time again" — but once firms successfully lobbied government, advantages became durable.

Fourth — and this is what you're missing — **the asymmetry is temporal, not permanent**. Ethisphere data shows that the "World's Most Ethical Companies" outperformed comparable large-cap companies by **24.6 percentage points** over five years. Unethical wealth is inherently unstable because it depends on ongoing deception. The market process tends to expose fraud over time. Trust compounds; fraud decays. You're observing a cross-sectional snapshot and mistaking it for a permanent structural law.

### Why dishonesty extracts more life per unit of value

In Böhm-Bawerk's framework, legitimate profit is compensation for postponing consumption — the capitalist-entrepreneur saves, pays laborers, waits, bears uncertainty. The fraudster skips the waiting and production steps entirely. Their "return on effort" appears higher because they aren't bearing the full costs of production — they're transferring costs to victims. Genuine exchange is positive-sum (both parties gain subjectively); manipulation is zero-sum or negative-sum. The manipulator's "efficiency" is illusory — it's the efficiency of taking versus making.

Yes, and the evidence is unambiguous. **Trust is infrastructure**: it reduces transaction costs, fosters loyalty, and creates a moat that unethical competitors literally cannot copy without exposing themselves. Patagonia saw a 35% increase in customer loyalty from ethical positioning. Buffett built his empire on a reputation for integrity that became his primary competitive moat — starting with $174,000 in 1956. The key Kirznerian insight: your analytical knowledge IS the entrepreneurial edge. You see patterns others miss. The question is whether you'll bury that talent or invest it.
